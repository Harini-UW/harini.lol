There's a version of me that goes through entire weeks without really being in them.

She wakes up, opens her phone, reads the same three apps in the same order. She eats while watching something. She studies while listening to something else. She's never fully anywhere. I know this version of myself very well because I've been her, on and off, for most of my life — and the disorienting thing is that from the inside, it doesn't feel like a problem. It just feels like normal.

That's the trap with autopilot. It doesn't announce itself. It's not dramatic. It's just a kind of low-grade absence that you only notice in retrospect, when you look back at a week and think: *where did I even go?*

---

## The experiment I've been running

This month I tried something small. Before I do anything — before I open a tab, pick up my phone, start a task — I pause for two seconds and say, internally: *I am choosing to do this.*

It sounds almost silly when I write it out. But it changed something.

Because a lot of the time, when I actually stopped to say that, I realized I *wasn't* choosing. I was just... doing. Momentum without intention. The next logical thing in a sequence I hadn't started on purpose.

Sometimes I'd say "I am choosing to open Instagram" and immediately close the app. Not because Instagram is bad, but because the truth was that I hadn't chosen it — habit had. And there's a difference.

---

## What I'm reading

I've been slowly working through *Meditations* by Marcus Aurelius. It's one of those books that sounds like everyone must have read it by now, so I assumed I had absorbed it culturally enough. I hadn't.

There's a line that keeps coming back to me:

> "The impediment to action advances action. What stands in the way becomes the way."

I've been thinking about this in the context of distraction. The moments where I most want to check out — when something is hard, or uncomfortable, or slow — those are exactly the moments where presence matters most. The discomfort is pointing at something worth sitting with.

---

## A small thing that helped

I started keeping a notebook open on my desk. Not to write in particularly — just to have there. Physical, analog, waiting. It's become a kind of anchor. When I'm spiraling in my head, I write a sentence. Just one. What I'm feeling, what I'm noticing, what I want.

It doesn't fix anything. But it makes me feel like I'm in conversation with myself instead of just at the mercy of my thoughts.

---

I don't have a tidy conclusion this month. I'm still figuring this out, same as always. But I think that's okay. The point was never to solve it — just to keep asking the question.

Thanks for being here.

— Harini
